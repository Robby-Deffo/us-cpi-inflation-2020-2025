# Part 1 Insights – CPI Inflation (Jan 2020 – Present)

## Abstract
This study examines the evolution of consumer prices in the U.S. since January 2020, focusing on three major categories—Food, Gasoline, and Shelter—alongside a California metro comparison. Using BLS CPI-U (NSA) data, percentage changes relative to the January 2020 baseline were computed and visualized. The findings highlight the multi-dimensional nature of inflation: volatile energy, steadily rising food, and persistently sticky shelter costs.

## Methodology
- Monthly CPI data (Jan 2020 – present).
- Categories: Food & Beverages, Gasoline (all types), Shelter.
- Comparison: Los Angeles metro vs. U.S. average.
- Baseline normalization at Jan 2020 = 0% change.

## Findings
- **Food:** A steady, cumulative rise over four years, materially increasing household grocery costs.
- **Gasoline:** Extreme volatility; largest spike mid-2022 (>80% above Jan 2020 baseline).
- **Shelter:** Persistent upward trend; increases were smaller month-to-month but highly cumulative.
- **California (LA):** Generally tracked above the U.S. average, driven largely by housing costs.

## Implications
- **Macro:** Gasoline shocks drive short-term volatility; shelter dominates long-term CPI stickiness.
- **Business:** Input cost planning must distinguish between volatile (fuel) and structural (shelter) inflation.
- **Policy:** Monetary policy effectiveness is constrained by lagged shelter adjustments.
- **Research:** Demonstrates value of both descriptive statistics and baseline indexing in communicating inflation.

## Limitations
- Focused on broad categories; subcategories (e.g., meats, electricity) not included.
- Not seasonally adjusted data; seasonality may affect interpretation.
- Metro comparison limited to Los Angeles.
